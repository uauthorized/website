A Tale of Two Movements

Is the Black Lives Matter movement a modern continuation of the 1960s Civil Rights Movement, or a fundamentally new initiative? This analysis explores the core goals, tactics, and structures of both movements to understand their connections and divergences. The conversation between Alicia and Mark highlights a common point of inquiry: while the fight for racial equality persists, the methods and organizational landscapes have evolved significantly.

The Core Mission

While both movements strive for racial equality and justice, their specific objectives were shaped by the pressing issues of their time. The Civil Rights Movement targeted legally sanctioned segregation, whereas Black Lives Matter confronts systemic racism in modern policing and criminal justice.

Civil Rights Movement (1950s-60s)

  • End Legal Segregation: Dismantle "separate but equal" laws (Jim Crow) in public spaces like schools, transportation, and restaurants.
  • Secure Voting Rights: Overcome barriers like poll taxes and literacy tests designed to disenfranchise African Americans, culminating in the Voting Rights Act of 1965.
  • Eliminate Employment Discrimination: Fight for equal opportunities in the workplace, a key component of the Civil Rights Act of 1964.

Black Lives Matter (2013-Present)

  • End Police Brutality: Bring national attention to and demand accountability for the deaths of Black individuals at the hands of law enforcement.
  • Challenge Systemic Racism: Address institutional inequalities in the criminal justice system, from racial profiling to mass incarceration.
  • Amplify Black Voices: Create campaigns and platforms that center Black experiences and advocate for political, economic, and social liberation.

Strategies for Change

Both movements utilize direct action to challenge the status quo. However, the tools and platforms have changed dramatically, with BLM leveraging social media to achieve a global reach that was unimaginable in the 1960s.

Nonviolent Civil Disobedience

A cornerstone of the CRM, this involved peacefully breaking unjust laws. Sit-ins at segregated lunch counters and the Montgomery Bus Boycott are prime examples.

Marches and Rallies

Both movements have used mass mobilization to demonstrate solidarity and pressure political leaders. The 1963 March on Washington is iconic, while BLM organized massive global protests in 2020.

Legal Challenges

Organizations like the NAACP were pivotal for the CRM, using the court system to dismantle segregation, famously in Brown v. Board of Education.

Boycotts

Economic pressure was a powerful tool for the CRM. Today, this tactic has evolved into calls for boycotts of corporations perceived as complicit in racial injustice.

Digital Activism

Unique to BLM, hashtags like #BlackLivesMatter organize protests, disseminate information, and share firsthand accounts of police encounters, bypassing traditional media gatekeepers.

High-Profile Campaigns

BLM excels at creating media campaigns that focus public attention on specific cases of injustice, building on the CRM's strategy of highlighting individual stories to illustrate a broader problem.

Leadership and Structure

Perhaps the most significant difference lies in organizational structure. The CRM was largely defined by centralized, often male-dominated leadership, while BLM operates as a decentralized network with an emphasis on inclusive, local leadership.

Civil Rights Movement

Characterized by hierarchical organizations like the Southern Christian Leadership Conference (SCLC) and the NAACP. Charismatic leaders such as Martin Luther King Jr. served as national spokespeople, unifying the movement's message and direction.

Black Lives Matter

Operates as a decentralized network of local chapters. This "leader-full" model is designed to empower local organizers and prevent the movement from being derailed by targeting a single leader. The national entity, the Black Lives Matter Global Network Foundation (BLMGNF), provides resources but does not dictate local actions.

Financial Scrutiny: A Point of Divergence

While the core message for racial justice resonates widely, the financial management of the Black Lives Matter Global Network Foundation (BLMGNF) has drawn criticism. This distinguishes the modern movement, where fundraising and organizational transparency are subject to intense public and media scrutiny in the digital age.

BLMGNF 2020 Donations Breakdown

In 2020, following the murder of George Floyd, the BLMGNF raised approximately $90 million. According to news reports and its own filings, the allocation of these funds became a source of controversy.

  • ~33% Distributed: Approximately $29.7 million was granted to various charities, partner organizations, and local BLM chapters.
  • ~67% Retained: The remaining majority of funds were retained for operating expenses, investments, consulting fees, and other costs, with reports highlighting multi-million dollar payments to individuals connected to the founders. The foundation later reported a deficit of over $8.5 million.

Source: Analysis based on reports from WPDE and other news outlets regarding BLMGNF's 2020 financial disclosures.

Synthesizing the Movements

A Continuation of the Struggle: The spirit and fundamental goal of BLM—the demand for Black people to be treated with dignity and to live free from systemic oppression—is a direct continuation of the centuries-long struggle for civil rights. The grievances have evolved from Jim Crow laws to issues of police brutality and mass incarceration, but the underlying fight for equality remains the same. The use of protests, boycotts, and direct action clearly echoes the tactics of the 1960s.

A Fundamentally Different Organization: Where BLM diverges sharply is in its structure and the context in which it operates. Its decentralized, technology-driven, and inclusive nature stands in contrast to the more hierarchical CRM. Furthermore, the controversies surrounding the BLMGNF's finances highlight a modern challenge. This allows for a distinction: one can support the movement's meaning—the decentralized, grassroots call for justice—while remaining critical of the national organization's financial practices and leadership decisions.

Ultimately, BLM can be seen as the next chapter in the Civil Rights Movement, adapted for the 21st century with new tools, new challenges, and a new organizational philosophy.
